A torus is a geometric surface which has dimensions defined by two radii: the major radius (R) and the minor radius (r). The major radius is the distance from the center of the torus' hole to the center of the circular tube. The minor radius is the radius of the circular tube. In simpler terms, a torus contains one large circle and many smaller circles as the vertical cross sections.

Simulating Brownian motion on the Torus $T^2$ is very similar to on the Sphere $S^2$. We can use Euler-Maruyama and tangent-space Gaussian noise to project each space back onto the torus for a certain number of walkers and time steps.

The project_to_manifold method in torus.py maps points back onto the torus. We must verify that it is working correctly before continuing to simulations and plots.

It is important to check whether points on, outside, and inside the torus get projected onto the torus surface. The method is simply called for a point that is already on the torus. To check points inside and outside the torus, we move a point already on the torus either inside or outside the torus. The normal vector points perpendicular to the torus surface at that point, so when it is computed, multipled by a constant, and then added/subtracted to the point already on the torus, it moves the point inward or outward the torus surface. Then, we can verify whether the updated point returns back to the torus when the project_to_manifold method is applied.

Finally, all points must satisfy the torus equation, expressed as

$$ (\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} - R)^2 + z^2 = r^2 $$

where $x, y, z$ are the Cartesian coordinates, $R$ is the major radius, and $r$ is the minor radius.

After verifying that the project_to_manifold method works as expected, we can begin to set up the Brownian motion simulation.

A torus is initialized with major radius $R$ of 3 and minor radius $r$ of 1. The Brownian motion simulation uses a time step of $dt = 0.01$ and runs for $T = 100000$ steps. The trajectory starts at the $(u, v)$ coordinates (0, 0). $u$ is the major angle, which represents the rotation around the main central hole of the torus. $v$ is the minor angle, which represents the rotation inside of the tube. Both angles have a range of 0 to $2\pi$.

The parametric equations to calculate the Cartesian coordinates from u, v are

$$ x = (R + r\cos(v))\cos(u) $$

$$ y = (R + r\cos(v))\sin(u) $$

$$ z = r\sin(v) $$

By using the parametrize method in torus.py, the $(u, v)$ coordinates are converted into $(x, y, z)$ coordinates. From the chosen values, the Cartesian coordinates are (4, 0, 0).

The simulation is run for $T = 100000$ time steps using the euler_maruyama_step method. The trajectory is stored in an array, which contains the Cartesian coordinates of every sampled point.

To later analyze the distribution of Brownian motion on the torus, the coordinates are converted into torus coordinates $(u, v)$. This is done using the formulas

$$ u = \operatorname{atan2}(y, x) $$

$$ v = \operatorname{atan2}\!\left(z, \sqrt{x^2+y^2} - R\right) $$

We use the modulo operator to map both angles into the interval

$$ [0, 2\pi) $$

This is done to have a consistent coordinate system for the empirical distributions and comparison to the theoretical densities.

To study the behavior of the Brownian motion, an empirical probability distribution is constructed from the $(u, v)$ coordinates. A histogram (with 50 bins in each direction) is used to count the density of different regions of the torus. A higher density correlates to more visits of that location.

The empirical probability distribution is found by normalizing the histogram. In other words, we calculate the relative frequency of each bin. This is the count of visits in the bin divided by the total number of visits.

The resulting heatmap represents the empirical density of the Brownian motion on the torus. If a region has a brigher color (larger density), it is visited frequently.

We can now compare the heatmap above to the theoretical probability distribution.

First, the histogram bin centers (for $u$ and $v$ separately) are calculated to create a meshgrid. This is done by adding the leftmost bin with the rightmost bin and dividing the sum by two.

Then, the theoretical density is calculated using the formula, which is expressed as

$$ p(u,v)=\frac{R+r\cos(v)}{4\pi^2R} $$

where $R$ is the major radius and $V$ is the poloidal angle (angle around tube of torus).

Each density is normalized to ensure they have the same scaled. Finally, both the empirical and theoretical densities are plotted on the heatmap.

# Overlay theoretical p(u, v)
def compute_densities(empirical_density, u_edges, v_edges):
    # Build theoretical grid
    u_centers = (u_edges[:-1] + u_edges[1:]) / 2
    v_centers = (v_edges[:-1] + v_edges[1:]) / 2
    U, V = np.meshgrid(u_centers, v_centers)

    # Compute theoretical density using formula
    theoretical_density = (R + r*np.cos(V)) / (4 * np.pi**2 * R)

    # Normalize densities
    empirical_density = empirical_density / np.sum(empirical_density)
    theoretical_density = theoretical_density / np.sum(theoretical_density)
    
    return empirical_density, theoretical_density, U, V

empirical_density, theoretical_density, U, V = compute_densities(empirical_density, u_edges, v_edges)

Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence is used to measure how close the empirical probability distribution is to the theoretical probability distribution. The formula to determine this value is

$$ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P \,\|\, Q) = \sum_{i,j} P_{ij} \log\!\left( \frac{P_{ij}}{Q_{ij}} \right) $$

where $P$ is the empirical density, $Q$ is the theoretical density, and $ij$ represents the $ij$th bin of the distribution. Since $log(0)$ is undefined, we only use empirical densities that are greater than 0.

If the KL divergence is exactly zero, the two probability distributions are identical in shape, orientation, and concentration at every coordinate.

The KL divergence varies depending on how long the simulation is run for (how many time steps are taken). An array of possible $T$ values is initialized and the empirical and theoretical densities are computed. For each $T$ value, the empirical distribution is built, normalized, and the KL divergence value is computed. Then, these are plotted.

As seen in the plot of KL divergence vs. time, as the number of time steps increases, the KL divergence decreases. In other words, as the simulation runs for a longer time period, the empirical probability distribution gets closer to the theoretical probability distribution. This is reasonable because for a longer time period, there are more points in the trajectory and hence a larger sample size. A larger sample size correlates to convergence, as seen in the statistical law called the Law of Large Numbers (LLN).
